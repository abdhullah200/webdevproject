Hunger in Sudan

Tackling food insecurity and building a better future.

Solutions

Hunger Situation in Sudan

Over 24.6 million people

are facing food insecurity due to conflict and economic collapse.

Children at Risk

1 in 3 children suffers from malnutrition and stunted growth.

Rural Areas Hit Hardest

Remote villages face severe food shortages and lack access to aid.

Causes of Hunger in Sudan

Ongoing Conflict

War between the SAF and RSF since 2023 has displaced millions, destroyed farmland, and blocked aid access.

Climate Change

Recurring droughts, floods, and erratic weather patterns have damaged crops and reduced harvests.

Economic Collapse

Inflation and poverty have made food unaffordable for millions, especially in rural areas.

Blocked Humanitarian Aid

Looting, attacks on aid workers, and roadblocks have slowed or stopped lifesaving food delivery.

Current Efforts & Organizations Helping

UN World Food Programme (WFP)

Provides emergency food packages, cash transfers, hot meals, and nutrition supplements to 3–7 million people monthly across Sudan.

Food & Agriculture Organization (FAO)

Distributes seeds and farming support to rebuild food systems; reached ~550,000 households with over 5,000 MT of seeds in 2024.

School Meal Programs (World Vision & UNICEF)

Feed over 140,000 children daily in Darfur, increasing enrollment and pass rates.

Emergency Response Rooms (ERRs)

Community-led volunteer kitchens in 8 states providing meals, clean water, and evacuation support to over 11.5 million people.

Solutions for the Future

Addressing hunger in Sudan requires a multi-faceted approach that combines immediate relief efforts with long-term development strategies. First and foremost, expanding and securing humanitarian aid corridors is crucial to ensure uninterrupted food delivery to affected populations, especially in conflict zones. Strengthening peacebuilding initiatives between warring factions will help create a stable environment that supports agricultural production and market functioning.

Climate-smart agriculture must be promoted to help farmers adapt to increasingly unpredictable weather patterns. This includes drought-resistant crops, sustainable water management, and investment in farming technology. Moreover, rebuilding local food systems through support for smallholder farmers will improve food availability and affordability. Economic recovery programs that focus on reducing inflation and increasing incomes will also empower families to access sufficient and nutritious food.

Education and awareness campaigns can improve nutrition knowledge and encourage dietary diversity, reducing malnutrition rates. Finally, increasing collaboration between international organizations, local governments, and community groups is essential to create effective, culturally appropriate, and sustainable solutions that empower Sudanese communities to overcome hunger and build resilience against future crises.
