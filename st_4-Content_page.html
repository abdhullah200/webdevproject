Hunger in Somalia

Tackling food insecurity and building a better future.

Solutions

Hunger Situation in Somalia

Severe Food Insecurity

Over 7 million people face acute food insecurity due to prolonged droughts, conflict, and displacement.

Children at High Risk

Nearly 1 in 4 children under five suffer from wasting and malnutrition, threatening their survival and development.

Impact of Displacement

Millions displaced internally and across borders face disrupted livelihoods and limited access to food and services.

Causes of Hunger in Somalia

Ongoing Conflict

Persistent armed conflict and insecurity hinder farming, markets, and humanitarian access.

Climate Change

Repeated droughts and erratic rainfall patterns severely reduce agricultural productivity and water availability.

Economic Collapse

High food prices, inflation, and limited income opportunities reduce access to nutritious food for many families.

Blocked Humanitarian Aid

Access to food and medical assistance is often obstructed by insecurity, roadblocks, and attacks on aid workers, slowing or stopping lifesaving deliveries.

Current Efforts & Organizations Helping

UN World Food Programme (WFP)

Delivers emergency food aid, cash transfers, and nutrition support to millions of vulnerable Somalis. WFP also supports school feeding, social safety nets, and logistics to reach hard-to-access areas.

Action Against Hunger

Provides nutrition services, clean water, sanitation, and livelihood support to over a million people, focusing on community-based resilience programs.

Save the Children

Offers critical health and nutrition services to children, treating malnutrition and supporting stabilization centers, though funding cuts threaten service continuity.

Local Initiatives

Community kitchens and food banks help displaced and food-insecure households, with coordination among agencies and local groups to improve aid delivery.

Solutions for the Future

Combating hunger in Somalia requires strengthening peace and security to enable stable livelihoods and humanitarian access. Expanding drought resilience programs, including water harvesting and climate-smart agriculture, will help communities adapt to changing conditions.

Increasing investment in local food systems and market access can improve food availability and affordability. Nutrition education and health services must be scaled up to reduce malnutrition, especially among children and mothers.

Empowering displaced populations through targeted social safety nets and livelihood support will build resilience and reduce vulnerability. Collaboration between international agencies, government, and local communities is essential to create sustainable, culturally appropriate solutions for a hunger-free Somalia.
