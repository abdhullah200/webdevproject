Hunger Situation in Ethiopia

Progress and Persistent Challenges

Zero Hunger (SDG 2) aims to end hunger, achieve food security, improve nutrition, and promote sustainable agriculture.

Hunger and Nutrition in Ethiopia

Over 15.8 million people are facing acute food insecurity in 2025, with rural and conflict-affected regions most impacted.

Barriers to Achieving Zero Hunger

Conflict and Displacement: Ongoing violence has displaced millions, disrupted agriculture, and limited humanitarian access.

Causes of Hunger in Ethiopia

Conflict and Insecurity

Ongoing conflict in regions such as Tigray, Amhara, Oromia, and Afar has displaced millions, disrupted agricultural activities, and limited humanitarian access.

Climate Shocks and Environmental Stress

Flooding and environmental degradation further undermine agricultural productivity and food security.

Economic Challenges

High food prices and inflation have reduced families’ ability to purchase nutritious food.

Current Efforts & Organizations Helping

UN World Food Programme (WFP)

WFP delivers emergency food, cash, and nutrition support to millions of Ethiopians each month, focusing on the most food-insecure and displaced populations.

Food & Agriculture Organization (FAO)

FAO provides seeds, fertilizers, and farming support to help families restore agricultural production and strengthen food security in conflict and drought-affected areas.

School Meal Programs (WFP, Plan International, Mary’s Meals, IFA)

School feeding initiatives ensure hundreds of thousands of children receive daily nutritious meals, improving school attendance and learning outcomes in crisis affected regions

Community Led Food and Safety Net Initiatives Community Led Food and Safety Net Initiatives

The Productive Safety Net Program (PSNP) and local community kitchens deliver cash, food, and meals to millions of vulnerable households, especially in rural and hard-hit areas.

Solutions for the Future

To overcome persistent hunger and vulnerability in Ethiopia, the future of food security must focus on expanding and modernizing the Productive Safety Net Program (PSNP). This means not only providing cash and food support, but also integrating nutrition education, health services, and climate-resilient public works. By investing in projects like watershed management, soil conservation, and water infrastructure, PSNP can help communities withstand droughts and floods. Moving towards digital payment systems and real time monitoring will further improve the efficiency and transparency of aid delivery, ensuring that support reaches those who need it most, even during emergencies.

At the same time, Ethiopia needs to promote climate smart and sustainable agriculture to boost food production and resilience. This involves supporting farmers with drought resistant seeds, training on sustainable farming methods, and expanding access to efficient irrigation and water management systems. Agroforestry and land restoration initiatives, such as the Green Legacy, can help restore degraded land, diversify crops, and secure long term food supplies for rural communities.

Finally, empowering local communities is key to lasting change. Expanding home grown school feeding programs by sourcing ingredients from local farmers can improve child nutrition and strengthen rural economies. Institutionalizing and scaling up community kitchens and food banks, linked with local producers and safety net programs, will ensure vulnerable families have reliable access to nutritious meals. By combining these approaches, Ethiopia can build a more resilient, self-sufficient, and hunger free future for all its people.
